A crime-data explorer

The justice funnel: from arrest to outcome

The maps and dashboards track the front of the system — the arrest. This one follows what happens after: of the cases New York City’s courts handle, how many are declined, dismissed, pled down, convicted and sentenced — and how that has shifted since New York’s bail laws changed on January 1, 2020.

How to read this

Two views, two clocks

Outcomes of disposed cases uses the state’s disposition tables: every adult arrest that reached a final outcome in a given year, and what that outcome was. It runs from 2014 to 2024, so it’s the view that spans the bail-reform line. But it counts by the year a case ended, not the year the arrest happened — a 2018 arrest resolved in 2020 lands in 2020.

The arraignment cohort uses the court system’s case-level pretrial data: every case first arraigned in a given year, followed forward. It’s the only view that shows what happened at arraignment — released on recognizance, bail set, remanded — the actual machinery bail reform changed. But it starts in 2020, so it has no pre-reform baseline, and recent years are incomplete: many 2024 felony cases were still open when the data was last published.

Neither view is a literal pipe from one arrest to one sentence. Arrest counts and disposition counts use different denominators and different years; treat the top of each funnel as its own starting universe, not a running total from the one above it.

What the data can and can’t say

  • “Pled down” is visible. Both sources record the conviction charge separately from the arrest charge, so a felony arrest that ends in a misdemeanor or non-criminal conviction shows up as exactly that — the single most common way a felony case resolves in conviction here.
  • Declined-to-prosecute is essentially a New York City phenomenon in this data, and it captures declinations at or after arraignment — cases a district attorney declines before arraignment never enter the court files at all, so true declination is undercounted.
  • 2020 is doubly disrupted. It is both the first year of bail reform and the year COVID closed the courts. The state warns against reading 2020 outcomes as a normal year; where you want a clean before/after, compare 2019 with 2021 and later.
  • Bail reform is not one event. The law took effect January 1, 2020 and was amended in 2020, 2022 and 2023. A single “before and after” line simplifies a moving target.
  • Recent felony cases are still open. In the arraignment-cohort view, a large share of the newest felony cases had no final disposition when the data was published; their outcomes are shown as pending, not as dismissals or acquittals.

Full methodology, category mapping and build code: see the repository and METHODOLOGY.md.

Everything substantive — the concept, the two-view design that keeps the data’s two different clocks honest, and the editorial judgment — was handled by the author (a human). The coding and data assembly were primarily in the hands of Anthropic’s Claude and Claude Code, under close direction. Quality controls included parsing only the state’s own published tables and the court system’s own case files, spot-checking parsed figures against the source spreadsheets cell by cell, following transferred felony cases by their case identifier so none is double-counted, and labeling every limit — disposition-year versus arrest-cohort, the 2020 COVID disruption, pending cases — on the page itself. Nevertheless, the risks of using AI remain, and we’d be grateful to learn of any red flags, errors or omissions you notice.
